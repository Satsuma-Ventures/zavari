## Playbook Context

Read the **Principles** and **Venture Lifecycle** sections of `PLAYBOOK.md` before beginning this phase. This skill consumes the Signal Brief produced by `1-plant/1-signal.md` and produces the Thesis Document consumed by `1-plant/3-concept.md`.

The principles most active in this phase:

- **Value created, not just captured** — the impact filter is a named section in the Thesis Document; a venture that only transfers value without creating it does not clear this bar
- **The AI-native advantage is real** — include as a named factor in the right-to-win assessment; quantify where possible (e.g. faster validation cycles, lower capital requirements)
- **Kill early, kill cleanly** — a Thesis Document that honestly identifies a fatal flaw is more valuable than one that papers over it

## Purpose

Turn the Signal Brief into a defensible investment thesis. The job is to map what already exists, size the opportunity, identify the specific gap, articulate what Satsuma believes that the market does not yet reflect, and surface the top risks honestly. The output is a document the founder would write to convince themselves — not a pitch deck, not a business plan.

---

## Behavior

### 1. Orient to the Signal Brief

Confirm you have reviewed the Signal Brief from Phase 1. Identify:

- The core problem and the user who has it
- The Key Assumption — this is the thread Phase 2 must pressure-test
- The net positive impact framing from Phase 1

If no Signal Brief exists, do not proceed. Ask the founder to complete Phase 1 first.

### 2. Map the competitive context

Use the **Satsuma Competitive Context Framework** — a four-ring model designed for venture validation, not for analyzing established businesses:

**Ring 1 — Direct competitors**
Products or services that explicitly solve the same problem for the same user. What do they do well? Where do they fall short? What is their business model and pricing? What do users say about them?

**Ring 2 — Indirect competitors**
Different solutions to the same underlying need — including doing nothing, manual workarounds, spreadsheets, or adjacent products that partially address the problem. These are often more important than direct competitors for an early-stage venture.

**Ring 3 — Emerging threats**
Companies or capabilities that do not compete today but could within 12–24 months. AI capabilities shifting, well-funded adjacent players, platform expansions. Be specific — "AI might do this someday" is not a threat; "OpenAI launched X in March 2025" is.

**Ring 4 — Structural forces**
Network effects, switching costs, regulatory dynamics, distribution moats, and other structural features that determine whether a new entrant can gain a foothold. What would make this market hard to enter? What would make it hard to hold once entered?

Present findings as a narrative. The goal is understanding the landscape, not producing a feature comparison matrix.

### 3. Size the opportunity

Produce a directional market sizing. The purpose is not precision — it is a sense-check on whether the opportunity is worth pursuing. Be explicit that these are estimates, not financial projections.

Structure as:

**Total Addressable Market (TAM)**
The full universe of people or organizations who have this problem, if a perfect solution existed. State the number and the reasoning behind it.

**Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)**
The portion of TAM that Satsuma could realistically reach given geography, go-to-market approach, and product scope. State the number and the constraints that define it.

**Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)**
A realistic capture estimate for years 1–3, given competitive dynamics, Satsuma's AI-native execution speed, and available distribution. State the number and the assumptions behind it.

If the market is too small to sustain a venture at SOM, say so directly. If the market is large but structurally difficult to penetrate, say that too.

### 4. Identify the gap

Based on the competitive context, articulate the specific gap this venture occupies:

- What do existing solutions not do?
- Which user needs are underserved, ignored, or poorly served?
- Is the gap a missing capability, a missing business model, a missing distribution approach, or a missing insight about the user?

Be specific. "No one does this well" is not a gap analysis. "Existing solutions require X, but the user actually needs Y, and no one has built for Y because of Z" is.

### 5. Formulate the thesis

Write the investment thesis as a set of beliefs. A thesis is not a description of what the product will do — it is a statement of what Satsuma believes to be true about the market, the user, and the timing that others have not yet acted on.

Structure as:
- **We believe** [market insight or user truth]
- **Because** [evidence or reasoning]
- **Which means** [the opportunity that follows]
- **And we are positioned to act because** [Satsuma's specific right to win, including AI-native execution advantage where relevant]

The thesis must be falsifiable. If there is no way to be wrong, it is not a thesis — it is a mission statement.

### 6. Evaluate net positive impact

Apply the Satsuma impact filter with specificity:

- Who benefits from this existing in the world, beyond the paying customer?
- Does solving this problem well make things genuinely better — not just more convenient for the person paying?
- Is there a meaningful negative externality this venture could create?

A venture that only transfers value from one party to another — without creating net new value for the world — does not pass the Satsuma filter. Surface this finding honestly if the thesis does not clear this bar.

### 7. Surface the top three risks

Identify the three risks most likely to kill this venture, ordered by severity:

For each risk:
- Name it clearly
- Explain why it is a genuine threat, not a hypothetical one
- Identify what would need to be true to mitigate it

Categories to consider: market timing, user behavior change required, technical feasibility, distribution challenge, regulatory exposure, competitive response, unit economics, founder/studio fit.

Three risks is the discipline. If there are more than three existential risks, that is itself a finding.

### 8. Assess right to win

Assess Satsuma's specific, defensible advantage for this venture:

- **Domain knowledge:** Does Satsuma understand this problem better than most teams starting from scratch?
- **Network access:** Does Satsuma have relationships that accelerate distribution, validation, or partnerships?
- **Timing insight:** Has Satsuma identified a window that others have not yet noticed?
- **AI-native execution:** Does Satsuma's ability to research, design, and build faster and cheaper than a traditional team create a meaningful head start? For what specific phases of this venture does that advantage matter most?

Be honest. A weak right to win does not automatically kill the idea, but it belongs in the risk register.

---

### 10. Present and discuss

Share the Thesis Document. Focus the discussion on:

- Does the gap description match what the founder believes?
- Does the market sizing feel directionally right?
- Does the thesis feel like something worth betting six months on?
- Are the risks correctly identified and ordered?
- Does the right to win feel earned?

Revise based on feedback. Do not proceed to Phase 3 until the founder explicitly confirms the Thesis Document is accurate.

### 11. Gate: Proceed or kill

> Based on this Thesis Document, do you want to proceed to Phase 3 — Concept — or kill/defer this idea?

If the founder proceeds: save the Thesis Document. Phase 3 will translate this thesis into a visual product concept.

If the founder kills or defers: document the reason.

## Guardrails

**Perspective:** Maintain the stance of a realistic, neutral third party. Directionally positive evidence should be presented as such. Directionally negative evidence should be presented as such. Neither should be softened or amplified.

**Do not soften findings.** If research contradicts the founder's assumptions from Phase 1, surface the contradiction directly. A Thesis Document that overstates the opportunity wastes Phase 3 effort and erodes trust in the process.

**The thesis must be falsifiable.** If it cannot be wrong, rewrite it.

**Market sizing is directional.** Do not present TAM/SAM/SOM numbers with false precision. State the estimates, state the reasoning, and be explicit about what you do not know.

**Do not anchor in traditional venture timelines.** Satsuma's AI-native model compresses execution. Right-to-win assessments should reflect realistic AI-accelerated timelines, not industry averages.

**Three risks, ordered by severity.** Not a comprehensive risk register — the three most likely to be fatal.

**Do not proceed without explicit founder confirmation.**

**The Thesis Document is one page.** If the thesis cannot be stated in one page, it is not clear enough.

**Phase boundary discipline.** Phase 2 produces a thesis and risk assessment. Phase 3 produces the concept, design system, and early user validation. Do not generate design direction, product concepts, or user validation requirements in Phase 2 — those are Phase 3's job. If the thesis work surfaces strong product intuitions, log them as a note ("Phase 3 should explore: [idea]") and stay at the thesis level.
