# Signal Brief: Zavari

*2026-06-24 · Author: Satsuma Ventures · Skill: 1-signal v1.3*

---

**The Problem**

Product leaders at growth-stage tech companies make consequential strategic decisions by synthesizing signals across many disconnected tools, with no system connecting that strategy to whether teams are actually executing against it — and no discipline for reasoning about the *second-order effects* of the direction they choose (the investment tradeoffs and opportunity costs, downstream product effects, and user impacts a decision sets in motion). The reasoning is undocumented, untraceable, and leaves when the leader does.

**Who Has It**

CPOs, VPs of Product, and Heads of Strategy at growth-stage product organizations. ICP stage is deliberately held open: the founder's most acute lived examples are later-stage, and the alignment value may *grow with* a company as it scales rather than sit at a fixed Series B–D band. Phase 2 sharpens the stage and the buyer.

**What They Do Today**

Synthesize manually — in one person's head, a three-hour offsite, or a deck built the night before. Increasingly they reach for bundled or general-purpose AI (Rovo, Copilot, Notion AI, ChatGPT) that answers the question they *think to ask*, from the data they think to surface, inside the tool they're already in. It does not triangulate across sources, run between planning cycles, reason about what a decision forecloses, or surface what no one thought to ask. The competitive baseline is the status quo and "good-enough AI" — not a named competitor.

**Why Now**

AI has made multi-source synthesis cheap, moving the bottleneck from "can we analyze the signals" to "are we choosing — and executing — the right strategy, and have we reasoned through what it sets in motion." That shift is what makes the differentiated opportunity *forward-looking*: predictive strategy and second-order-effect reasoning (decision tradeoffs, opportunity cost, downstream consequences), not backward-looking synthesis — the part that is rapidly commoditizing. a16z's Institutional-vs-Individual-AI framing names the broader gap; multiple funded entrants are moving on "deciding what to build" as the high-value AI bottleneck — but on the synthesis side, not the predictive/decision-consequence side.

**Net Positive Impact**

*Deferred and decoupled — not yet pressure-tested, and deliberately not fused with the opportunity.* On its own honest terms the surface answer is currently weak: primary benefit accrues to well-capitalized companies, with less wasted engineering effort as the main second-order benefit and no obvious societal externality. Per founder direction, this is left as a standalone question for Phase 2's impact filter rather than retrofitted into the wedge as a positioning narrative.

**Why Satsuma**

Genuine founder domain expertise in exactly this problem — senior product leadership at Expedia, Virtuoso, and Porch, with the triangulation framework derived from lived practice (Dell/Chorhummel) and a physical analogue at Magellan GPS — plus AI-native execution speed. Counterweighted honestly: the headline mechanism (cross-source triangulation) is commoditizing, so the credible edge is *practitioner insight into where the real, unowned seam is* — predictive / second-order strategic reasoning and the strategy-to-execution bridge — not the synthesis technology itself.

**The Key Assumption**

That a defensible, buyable wedge exists in the unowned, forward-looking seam — most likely *predictive strategy and second-order decision-consequence reasoning* (tradeoffs, opportunity cost, downstream product and user effects) bridged to execution alignment — and that the pain is acute enough that a specific buyer changes behavior to adopt it. Everything turns on this. Phase 2 pressure-tests it; Track 1 ultimately validates it via the Phase 3 concept.

**Phase 2 Agenda** *(head-start notes from Phase 1 research — logged, not gated)*

- **Wedge candidate:** test predictive / second-order decision-consequence reasoning (internal strategic tradeoffs, opportunity cost, downstream effects) as the differentiated wedge, distinct from the commoditizing backward-looking synthesis incumbents are racing toward. *This is the Key Assumption and the primary thing Phase 2 must research fresh.*
- **Competitive convergence / timing:** Productboard Spark (GA ~June 2026) markets the same synthesis stack; Amplitude already holds two of three triangulation legs plus execution write-back. White-space window on synthesis is narrowing.
- **Moat:** the Triangulation Engine is commoditizing as AI makes multi-source synthesis cheap — locate durable defensibility (predictive layer, learning layer, or the seam).
- **Adjacent category risk:** the strategy-execution / OKR category is mature, churny, and crowded (Cagan de-endorsed OKRs; Viva Goals retired; Brev funded April 2026 for a near-identical alignment pitch).
- **GTM / business model:** $25–60K ACV is the least capital-efficient B2B band; solo non-technical founder + complex enterprise AI build is a stacked-low-base-rate execution profile.
- **The crux:** whether strategic misalignment / decision quality has a real software-addressable slice, or is fundamentally a leadership / operating-model problem software cannot fix (strong expert consensus leans toward the latter).
- **Net positive impact:** assess on its own terms in Phase 2 — do not shoehorn into the opportunity.

**Recommendation**

**Proceed to Phase 2 — Thesis.** It clears the narrow Phase 1 gate: the problem is real and research-confirmed, the user is specific, and there is a credible right to pursue. The substantial open questions — the predictive wedge, moat, timing, business model, and the tooling-vs-dysfunction crux — are Phase 2's job, and Phase 1 has produced a strong head start on all of them.
